What is an ECG?

An Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG) is a medical test that records the electrical activity of your heart. It shows how fast your heart is beating and whether its rhythm is steady or irregular.

The ECG waveform consists of several components: P wave, QRS complex, and T wave, each representing different phases of the cardiac cycle.

ECG Waveform Components

  • P Wave: Represents atrial depolarization (contraction)
  • QRS Complex: Represents ventricular depolarization
  • T Wave: Represents ventricular repolarization
  • RR Interval: Time between consecutive heartbeats

Normal Heart Rate

A normal resting heart rate for adults ranges from 60 to 100 beats per minute (bpm).

  • • Bradycardia: Heart rate below 60 bpm
  • • Normal: Heart rate 60-100 bpm
  • • Tachycardia: Heart rate above 100 bpm

Common Arrhythmias

PVC (Premature Ventricular Contraction)

An extra heartbeat originating from the ventricles, marked as V on ECG

SVE (Supraventricular Ectopy)

Abnormal beats originating above the ventricles, marked as S on ECG

Fusion Beats

Hybrid beats combining features of normal and abnormal beats, marked as F on ECG

Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV measures the variation in time between consecutive heartbeats. Higher variability is generally associated with better cardiovascular health.

Key Metrics:

  • • SDNN: Standard deviation of RR intervals (normal: 50-100ms)
  • • RMSSD: Root mean square of successive differences
  • • pNN50: Percentage of RR intervals differing by >50ms
